# Adult Dataset Privacy Analysis Rationale

These notes explain the design choices used for the Adult dataset anonymization analysis. They are written to support the assignment report, not to replace the numeric results generated by `scripts/adult_privacy_analysis.py`.

## Attribute Classification

| Attribute | Classification | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| `age` | QID | Age is commonly known or guessable and strongly separates records when combined with work and demographic fields. |
| `workclass` | QID | Employment sector may be known from public or social context and helps distinguish people. |
| `fnlwgt` | Insensitive/excluded | This is a Census sampling weight, not a direct personal characteristic for release. It has many values, but it is not useful as a public analytical attribute here. |
| `education` | QID | Education level is often known and helps identify smaller groups. |
| `education-num` | Insensitive/excluded | It duplicates `education` numerically. Keeping both would count the same information twice. |
| `marital-status` | QID | Marital status is demographic background information that can be known externally. |
| `occupation` | QID | Occupation is often public or inferable and has strong separation power. |
| `relationship` | QID | Household role helps distinguish people when combined with marital status, age, and sex. |
| `race` | QID | Race helps linkage because it separates the dataset into demographic groups. In other contexts it may also be treated as sensitive or protected. |
| `sex` | QID | Sex is often known and contributes to linkage, even though alone it has low distinction. |
| `capital-gain` | Sensitive | It reveals financial information. Exact financial values are private outcomes, not likely attacker background knowledge. |
| `capital-loss` | Sensitive | It reveals financial information and should not be used for linkage. |
| `hours-per-week` | QID | Work hours may be known or inferable and has many values, so it increases distinction. |
| `native-country` | QID | Country of origin is demographic information that can be externally known and can separate minority groups. |
| `income` | Sensitive | Income is the private fact to protect. It is the dataset target and the clearest sensitive attribute. |

There are no direct identifying attributes because the dataset does not contain names, addresses, phone numbers, email addresses, IDs, or account numbers.

## Why These Roles And Not Others

QIDs are not identifiers by themselves. The privacy risk comes from combinations. For example, `age = 41`, `sex = Female`, `occupation = Prof-specialty`, `native-country = Portugal`, and `education = Doctorate` may describe a much smaller group than any one field alone. That is why the analysis considers both distinction and separation:

- Distinction means how many different values or value combinations an attribute creates.
- Separation means how strongly records are split apart into different groups.

`race` and `sex` are classified as QIDs in this release scenario because they are plausible attacker background knowledge. This does not mean they are never sensitive. In a fairness, legal, or anti-discrimination analysis, they may be protected or sensitive attributes too.

`income` is classified as sensitive rather than as a QID because the attack of concern is learning someone's income class. If an attacker already knows a person's income, protecting `income` in this dataset would not make much sense; but for this assignment, income is the private outcome.

`capital-gain` and `capital-loss` are classified as sensitive because they reveal financial status. They are not used as QIDs because an attacker is less likely to know exact capital gain or loss values before the attack. Treating them as QIDs would also create very small groups and force heavy generalization or suppression.

`education-num` is excluded because it is a numeric version of `education`. Keeping both `education` and `education-num` would exaggerate the importance of education in the risk calculation without adding real information.

`fnlwgt` is excluded because it is a survey weight used by the Census process. It is not a meaningful characteristic to publish for individual-level analysis in this assignment.

## Disclosure Concepts

Identity disclosure happens when an attacker links a released row to a specific person.

Simple example: suppose a released record has `age = 52`, `sex = Female`, `education = Doctorate`, and `native-country = Portugal`. If only one person in the dataset has that combination and the attacker knows those facts about Maria, the attacker can link the row to Maria.

Attribute disclosure happens when an attacker learns a sensitive value about a person, even if the exact row is not uniquely identified.

Simple example: suppose Maria is known to be in a QID group of five people. If all five records in that group have `income = >50K`, the attacker learns Maria's income class even though they cannot identify which of the five rows is hers.

## Anonymization Model Options

### k-Anonymity

`k`-anonymity requires each released QID pattern to appear in at least `k` records. If `k = 5`, every combination of generalized QID values must describe at least five records.

Simple example:

| Age | Sex | Education | Income |
|---|---|---|---|
| 30-39 | Female | Bachelors | <=50K |
| 30-39 | Female | Bachelors | >50K |
| 30-39 | Female | Bachelors | <=50K |
| 30-39 | Female | Bachelors | <=50K |
| 30-39 | Female | Bachelors | >50K |

This group satisfies `5`-anonymity because five records share the same QID values.

Pros:

- Easy to understand and explain.
- Directly reduces identity disclosure risk.
- Gives a clear parameter: larger `k` means larger groups.

Cons:

- Does not guarantee sensitive-value protection.
- A group can satisfy `k`-anonymity while all records share the same income.
- Larger `k` usually requires more generalization or suppression.

### l-Diversity

`l`-diversity requires each QID group to contain at least `l` different sensitive values. In this analysis, `l = 2` for the binary `income` attribute, so each released group must contain both `<=50K` and `>50K`.

Simple example:

| Age | Sex | Education | Income |
|---|---|---|---|
| 40-49 | Male | HS-grad | <=50K |
| 40-49 | Male | HS-grad | <=50K |
| 40-49 | Male | HS-grad | >50K |
| 40-49 | Male | HS-grad | <=50K |
| 40-49 | Male | HS-grad | >50K |

This group satisfies distinct `2`-diversity because it has two income values.

Pros:

- Addresses attribute disclosure better than `k`-anonymity alone.
- Simple to explain when the sensitive attribute is categorical.
- Useful for showing why identity protection and sensitive-value protection are different.

Cons:

- With binary `income`, `l` cannot be meaningfully higher than 2.
- It only checks that values are present, not whether the distribution is close to normal.
- It can still allow skewed groups, such as 99 records with `<=50K` and 1 with `>50K`.

### t-Closeness

`t`-closeness requires the sensitive-value distribution inside each QID group to be close to the sensitive-value distribution in the full dataset. Smaller `t` is stricter.

Simple example: if the full dataset is about 76% `<=50K` and 24% `>50K`, a QID group with 75% `<=50K` and 25% `>50K` is close. A group with 100% `>50K` is far from the overall distribution and may fail `t`-closeness.

Pros:

- Stronger than l-diversity against skewed sensitive-value distributions.
- Protects against learning that a group is unusual with respect to income.
- Good for comparing privacy and utility tradeoffs because the `t` parameter can be varied.

Cons:

- More complex to explain than `k`-anonymity or `l`-diversity.
- Stricter values can require heavy generalization or suppression.
- Requires a distance measure between distributions.

### Suppression-Only

Suppression removes or hides records or values that create privacy risk.

Simple example: if only one record has `age = 66`, `occupation = Armed-Forces`, and `native-country = Cambodia`, that record could be suppressed instead of generalized.

Pros:

- Easy to understand.
- Can remove rare high-risk records quickly.
- Preserves exact values for records that remain.

Cons:

- Can remove too many records.
- Can bias the dataset if rare groups are systematically removed.
- Does not create a useful hierarchy-based explanation by itself.

### Generalization-Only

Generalization replaces detailed values with broader categories without removing rows.

Simple example: `age = 37` becomes `age = 35-39`, then `age = 30-39`, then `age = adult`.

Pros:

- Preserves row count.
- Makes the transformation easy to describe.
- Works naturally with ARX-style coding models and hierarchies.

Cons:

- Can erase useful detail.
- Rare combinations may still remain rare unless generalized heavily.
- Can make analysis less precise.

## Why The Two Models Were Chosen

The two chosen models are:

- Model A: `k`-anonymity + distinct `l`-diversity on `income`.
- Model B: `k`-anonymity + `t`-closeness on `income`.

They were chosen because the assignment asks for two anonymization models and risk/utility comparison. Both models start with `k`-anonymity, so they address identity disclosure. They then differ in how they address attribute disclosure:

- `l`-diversity checks that each group has more than one income value.
- `t`-closeness checks that each group's income distribution is close to the full dataset's income distribution.

This gives a useful comparison: Model A is simpler and easier to explain, while Model B is stricter and more sensitive to distribution skew.

## Global Recoding Hierarchies

Global recoding means the same generalization rule is applied everywhere in the dataset. If `age = 37` is generalized to `35-39`, then every record with age 37 is generalized the same way.

Examples:

- `37` -> `35-39` -> `30-39` -> `adult` -> `*`
- `Portugal` -> `Europe` -> `Non-US` -> `*`
- `Bachelors` -> `bachelors` -> `tertiary` -> `*`
- `Prof-specialty` -> `professional-managerial` -> `white-collar` -> `*`

The benefit is consistency. A report can explain the coding model clearly, and the same hierarchy can be reused across privacy models.

The cost is flexibility. Local recoding could generalize one age value differently in different parts of the dataset, potentially preserving more detail. Global recoding is less flexible, so it may lose more utility, but it is easier to justify and audit.

## Risk Metrics

Fraction of unique QID records measures how many records are alone in their QID group. A high value means many records are easy to link.

Maximum prosecutor risk is `1 / min_equivalence_class_size`. It models an attacker who knows the target is in the dataset. If the smallest group has one record, the maximum risk is 100%. If the smallest group has five records, it is 20%.

Average equivalence-class risk summarizes the overall linkage risk across all groups. It is lower when records are spread across larger equivalence classes.

Suppression rate measures how many records must be hidden or removed to satisfy the privacy model. It is both a privacy mechanism and a utility cost.

These metrics were chosen because the assignment explicitly asks for re-identification risk before and after anonymization. They also match the intuition of equivalence classes used by `k`-anonymity.

## Utility Metrics

Normalized generalization loss measures how far each QID moved up its hierarchy. Exact values have low loss; `*` has maximum loss.

Total information loss combines generalization and suppression. It reflects both loss of detail and loss of records.

Discernibility penalty penalizes small equivalence classes and suppressed records. It is useful because a dataset with many tiny groups is less anonymous than one with larger groups.

Distribution drift compares important distributions before and after anonymization. The analysis checks `income`, `age`, `education`, `occupation`, `race`, `sex`, and `native-country` because these are important for the Adult dataset's analytical value.

These metrics were chosen because privacy alone is not enough. A dataset can be very private if everything is replaced with `*`, but then it is not useful. Utility metrics show how much analytical value remains after anonymization.

## Short Report-Ready Summary

The Adult dataset has no direct identifiers, but it contains many quasi-identifiers. Demographic and work attributes such as age, occupation, education, sex, race, and native country can distinguish and separate records when combined. Income and financial gain/loss fields are sensitive because they reveal private economic information. The analysis therefore protects identity disclosure through `k`-anonymity and protects attribute disclosure through either `l`-diversity or `t`-closeness. Global recoding hierarchies make the anonymization consistent and explainable. Risk is measured using QID uniqueness and equivalence-class risk, while utility is measured through information loss, suppression, discernibility, and distribution drift.
